Designing a Health Companion Super App

A user-centered approach to helping individuals with chronic health conditions manage medications, appointments, and communication with healthcare professionals.

Role
UX/UI Designer

01 — Overview

The Problem

Millions of people living with chronic conditions struggle to manage complex medication regimens and coordinate care across multiple healthcare providers. Existing solutions are fragmented, clinical-feeling, and fail to account for the emotional and cognitive load these patients carry daily.

Our design thinking process: Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test

Design Challenge: How might we create a mobile health management experience that feels supportive rather than clinical, reduces cognitive load for users managing multiple medications, and seamlessly connects patients with their healthcare providers?

02 — User Research

Understanding Our Users

To design a truly user-centered solution, I employed a multi-method research approach combining qualitative and quantitative techniques to build a deep understanding of our target users' needs, behaviors, and pain points.

Research Methods

Semi-Structured Interviews

Conducted 12 in-depth interviews (45-60 minutes each) with individuals managing chronic conditions including diabetes, hypertension, and asthma. Key topics explored:

  • Daily medication management routines and challenges
  • Experience with existing health apps and tools
  • Communication patterns with healthcare providers
  • Emotional impact of managing chronic conditions
  • Technology comfort level and preferences

Online Survey

Distributed a survey to 150 respondents via online health communities and patient advocacy groups. The survey gathered quantitative data on:

  • Medication adherence rates and common barriers
  • Feature preferences for a health management app
  • Current tools used (paper, apps, pill organizers)
  • Frequency of missed doses and consequences
  • Willingness to share health data digitally

Competitive Analysis

Analyzed 8 existing health management apps (Medisafe, MyTherapy, CareZone, Healthily, etc.) to identify strengths, weaknesses, and market gaps:

  • Most apps focus on medication tracking only
  • Poor integration between medication and appointment features
  • Limited or no direct doctor communication
  • Clinical, sterile visual design that feels impersonal
  • Overly complex onboarding processes

Secondary Research

Reviewed academic literature and industry reports on medication adherence, mHealth adoption, and UX design for older adults:

  • WHO reports on medication adherence in chronic disease
  • Nielsen Norman Group research on senior-friendly design
  • Studies on push notification fatigue in health apps
  • HIPAA and health data privacy best practices
  • Accessibility standards (WCAG 2.1 AA) for health apps

03 — User Personas

Who Are We Designing For?

Based on the research findings, I developed three detailed personas representing the core user segments of the HealthCompanion app. These personas guided every design decision throughout the project.

Primary Persona: The Independent Senior

Sarah Chen — Retired Teacher, Age 62

Scenario: Sarah was diagnosed with Type 2 diabetes and hypertension 5 years ago. She takes 4 daily medications and sees 3 specialists regularly. She lives alone in a suburban area and uses her smartphone primarily for calls, photos, and basic apps. She frequently forgets her afternoon medication because she gets absorbed in her gardening and book club activities.

How she would use the app: Sarah would rely heavily on medication reminders timed to her daily routine (breakfast, lunch, dinner), use the appointment scheduler to keep track of her various specialist visits, and occasionally message her endocrinologist about blood sugar concerns. She values the health dashboard to see her medication adherence at a glance and shares monthly reports with her daughter who lives out of state.

Needs Simplicity Large Text Routine-Based Reminders Family Sharing

Secondary Persona: The Busy Professional

Marcus Williams — Software Engineer, Age 34

Scenario: Marcus has had asthma since childhood and developed seasonal allergies in his 20s. He takes 2 daily maintenance medications and carries a rescue inhaler. His demanding work schedule means he often gets absorbed in deep focus sessions and misses his medication times. He's tried several health apps but found them too clinical or annoying with constant notifications.

How he would use the app: Marcus wants discrete, smart reminders that integrate with his calendar and work schedule. He prefers to book appointments quickly (ideally in under 30 seconds), and wants to message his doctor about allergy flare-ups instead of scheduling unnecessary office visits. He appreciates data visualization and wants to correlate his symptom patterns with environmental data.

Efficiency-First Calendar Integration Data Visualization Minimal Notifications

Tertiary Persona: The Caregiver

Maria Rodriguez — Caregiver & Part-time Nurse, Age 45

Scenario: Maria manages her own rheumatoid arthritis while caring for her 72-year-old mother with heart disease. She coordinates 5 medications for herself and 7 for her mother, across 4 different doctors. She has accidentally mixed up their medications twice in the past year, which frightened her deeply. She sometimes needs information in Spanish for her mother.

How she would use the app: Maria would use multi-profile support to manage both her own and her mother's medications separately but within the same app. She needs clear visual differentiation between profiles to prevent mix-ups, medication interaction warnings, and the ability to coordinate appointment schedules for both. She would use the messaging feature to ask her mother's cardiologist quick questions and relies on bilingual support.

Multi-Profile Drug Interaction Alerts Bilingual Support Clear Differentiation

06 — User Flow

Task Analysis & User Flow

Mapping the complete application flow helped identify optimal navigation paths, reduce unnecessary steps, and ensure every core task could be completed with minimal friction and cognitive load.

Key Task Flows

  • Medication reminder — 2 taps to confirm (notification → mark as taken)
  • Appointment booking — 3 steps (search → select doctor & time → confirm)
  • Messaging a doctor — 3 steps (open messages → select provider → compose & send)
  • Add medication flow — guided entry with barcode scanning shortcut and autocomplete drug names

Design Principles from Flow Analysis

  • 3-tap rule — every primary task reachable within 3 taps from the home screen
  • Binary decisions — each screen presents one clear action to minimize decision fatigue
  • Consistent back-navigation — predictable back button and swipe-to-go-back on every screen
  • Guided onboarding — progressive setup that introduces features gradually rather than all at once
07 — Wireframes

Low-Fidelity Wireframes

Starting with low-fidelity wireframes allowed me to quickly explore layout options, validate information architecture, and iterate on core user flows before investing in visual design. These wireframes focus on structure, hierarchy, and user flow.

Information Architecture

The app is organized around five primary navigation areas, designed to reduce cognitive load and provide quick access to the most frequently used features:

  • Home Dashboard — At-a-glance view of today's medications, upcoming appointments, and health summary
  • Medications — Complete medication list, adherence history, refill tracking, and drug interaction warnings
  • Appointments — Browse doctors, schedule appointments, view calendar, and manage visit history
  • Messages — Secure chat with healthcare providers, message history, and file attachments
  • Profile — Personal health info, settings, notification preferences, and family profiles

Key Screen Wireframes

Home Screen — Dashboard with today's medications and upcoming appointments
Medication Reminder — Clear action buttons for taking or snoozing medication
Appointment Booking — Search, browse doctors, select time slots
Doctor Messaging — Secure chat with healthcare providers

Wireframe Design Decisions

Card-Based Layout

The home screen uses a card-based layout to group related information (medications, appointments, health stats). This reduces visual clutter and allows users to quickly scan for what matters most. Cards also create a clear visual hierarchy, guiding the eye from most urgent (today's medications) to supplementary information.

Single-Action Principle

Each screen is designed around a primary action. The medication reminder screen has one prominent "Mark as Taken" button. The appointment screen guides users linearly from search to selection to confirmation. This reduces decision paralysis, especially important for our senior persona (Sarah) who may feel overwhelmed by too many options.

Bottom Navigation

A persistent bottom navigation bar with 5 items provides direct access to all primary sections. Icons are paired with text labels for clarity (critical for accessibility). The active state is clearly differentiated with color. This pattern is familiar to both iOS and Android users, reducing the learning curve.

Progressive Disclosure

Complex information is revealed progressively. The home screen shows a medication summary; tapping reveals full details. The messaging screen shows recent conversations; tapping opens the full chat. This keeps initial views clean while making detailed information accessible when needed.

Typography

Primary Font: Inter (sans-serif) — Highly legible at all sizes, with excellent readability for older users. Chosen for its large x-height and open letterforms.

Sizing Scale: Base size of 16px with a 1.25 modular scale. All text meets WCAG AA contrast requirements. Important information uses 18px+ for enhanced readability.

Spacing & Layout

Touch Targets: Minimum 44x44px for all interactive elements (WCAG recommendation), with most buttons using 48px+ height.

Border Radius: 16px for cards, 12px for buttons, 8px for input fields. Rounded corners feel friendlier and less clinical than sharp edges.

09 — Design Iterations

Iterations & Failed Designs

Great design comes from learning what doesn't work. Each iteration brought us closer to a solution that truly serves our users. Here are the key design changes driven by testing, feedback, and critical evaluation of earlier concepts.

Home Screen Iteration

Home Screen: Stats First vs. Tasks First

What failed: The initial design placed a health statistics summary (adherence percentage, weekly chart) at the top of the home screen. During testing, users consistently scrolled past it to find their immediate tasks — medications due now and upcoming appointments. The stats created unnecessary cognitive load at the moment users needed to take action.

The fix: Moved today's medication cards and upcoming appointments to the top of the home screen, placing the health summary and adherence stats below the fold. Users can still access their data, but the primary view now prioritizes actionable items. Task completion rates improved from 78% to 95% after this change.

Medication Reminder Iteration

Medication Reminder: Single Action vs. Flexible Response

What failed: The first medication reminder design featured a single large "Done" button with no alternative actions. During usability testing, 3 out of 6 participants expressed frustration when they couldn't take their medication immediately (waiting for food, in a meeting, away from home). They either force-tapped "Done" inaccurately or dismissed the notification entirely, breaking their adherence tracking.

The fix: Added a "Snooze 15 min" option alongside the primary "Mark as Taken" button, plus a "Skip — Log Reason" option for full transparency. The snooze button uses a secondary visual style so it doesn't compete with the primary action. This preserved data accuracy while respecting real-life constraints.

Appointment Booking Iteration

Appointment Booking: 5-Step Flow vs. 3-Step Flow

What failed: The original appointment booking flow required 5 separate screens: (1) select specialty, (2) browse doctors, (3) select doctor, (4) pick date & time, (5) confirm. Analytics from prototype testing showed a 40% drop-off between steps 3 and 4, and Marcus's persona type consistently expressed frustration with the length of the process.

The fix: Consolidated to 3 steps by combining doctor selection with available time slots on a single screen, and merging the specialty filter into a chip-based search bar on the first screen. The flow became: (1) search with specialty filters, (2) select doctor & time slot, (3) confirm. Booking completion rate increased from 60% to 92%, and average time-on-task dropped from 45 seconds to 18 seconds.

Analysis Methodology

Feedback was analyzed using a combination of:

  • Affinity Mapping: Organized all observations and quotes into thematic clusters using Miro, identifying common patterns across participants.
  • Severity Rating: Each issue was rated on a 1-4 severity scale (Nielsen's severity ratings) to prioritize fixes. Critical and major issues were addressed immediately; minor issues were logged for future iterations.
  • Task Analysis: Recorded the exact paths users took through the prototype, comparing against the intended "happy path" to identify where users deviated or got confused.
  • Quantitative Metrics: Calculated task completion rates, average time-on-task, and error rates to supplement qualitative observations.
11 — Reflection

Lessons Learned

Reflecting on the end-to-end design process reveals how deeply user-centered methods shaped the final product, and the challenges that forced creative problem-solving.

How UX Design Helped Identify User Needs

The UX design process was instrumental in uncovering needs that would have been invisible through assumptions alone. Three moments stand out:

  • The caregiver use case emerged from interviews. Our initial brief focused on individual patients, but 4 out of 12 interview participants mentioned managing medications for a family member. This led to the creation of Maria's persona and the multi-profile feature, which became a key differentiator from competitors.
  • Emotional mapping revealed the importance of positive reinforcement. The user journey map highlighted that users' emotions dip during daily use due to "notification fatigue." This insight drove the design of adherence streaks and the celebratory 92% ring on the dashboard, transforming medication management from a chore into an achievement.
  • Usability testing caught a critical flow issue. Three out of six testers hesitated at the medication reminder screen because the snooze option was not visually prominent enough. Without testing, we might have shipped a design where users felt forced to mark medications as taken even when they couldn't take them immediately, leading to inaccurate data and frustration.

Challenges Encountered

Balancing Simplicity vs. Feature Richness

The biggest tension was between Sarah's need for simplicity and Marcus's desire for data visualization. I resolved this through progressive disclosure: the default view is clean and simple, but power users can access detailed analytics by tapping into deeper screens. This "easy to learn, hard to master" approach serves both personas without compromise.

Designing for Accessibility Without Stigma

Making the app accessible for older users (large text, simple navigation) risked making younger users feel the app was "not for them." I overcame this by adopting a modern, clean design language that naturally incorporates accessibility features. Large touch targets and clear typography benefit all users, not just seniors, aligning with universal design principles.

Notification Sensitivity

Research showed users are both dependent on reminders and annoyed by them. The solution was smart, context-aware notifications that adapt to user behavior: if a user consistently takes medication at 8:05 AM, the app adjusts the reminder from the default 8:00 AM. Users also have granular control over notification frequency and style (sound, vibration, silent banner).

Trust and Privacy Concerns

Multiple participants expressed anxiety about storing health data in an app. I addressed this by making privacy visible: a dedicated privacy section in onboarding, clear HIPAA compliance badges, and transparent data usage explanations. The design communicates trustworthiness through professional visual design and explicit security indicators.

Impact of Iterative Design

The iterative nature of the design thinking process was the most valuable aspect of this project. Each phase built on discoveries from the previous one:

  • Research → Personas: Interview insights directly shaped the three distinct personas, ensuring we didn't design for a generic "user" but for real people with specific needs.
  • Personas → Journey Map: Sarah's persona drove the journey map's emotional dimension, revealing that the onboarding phase is where most anxiety exists.
  • Journey Map → Wireframes: Pain points identified in the journey (tedious data entry, notification fatigue) directly informed wireframe decisions (barcode scanning, smart reminders).
  • Wireframes → Testing: Low-fidelity wireframes allowed rapid iteration without attachment to visual design, making it easier to discard ideas that didn't work.
  • Testing → Final Design: Real user feedback transformed assumptions into validated decisions, resulting in a more intuitive and empathetic final product.

Final Thought: The most important lesson from this project is that good UX design isn't about making things look beautiful — it's about making people feel understood. Every design decision in HealthCompanion, from the warm greeting on the home screen to the celebratory adherence streak, was shaped by listening to real users and iterating based on their feedback. The result is an app that doesn't just manage medications — it supports people in managing their health with confidence and dignity.
